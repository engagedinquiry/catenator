# Adding views

A descriptor without a view is a label. A descriptor with a view is a question answered.

Views are added to a spec entry by introducing a `view` key within the descriptor block. A descriptor can carry one view or all five — apply the views the system's complexity and stakes require.

---

## Adding a view to a descriptor

Starting from the minimal spec, add a view to the `utility` descriptor:

```yaml
utility:
  - name: publish satirical jargon definitions
    for: reader
    produces: card-format definitions of business jargon
    delivers: physical and digital products via direct and wholesale channels
    view:
      interface:
        actor: reader
        context: >
          Reader encounters SIC products through retail, direct purchase,
          or as a gift. May have no prior knowledge of the imprint.
        situation: belongs — the product is acquired and kept
```

The view block names which view is being applied and provides the content for that view. A descriptor can carry multiple view entries:

```yaml
utility:
  - name: publish satirical jargon definitions
    for: reader
    produces: card-format definitions of business jargon
    delivers: physical and digital products via direct and wholesale channels
    view:
      interface:
        actor: reader
        context: >
          Reader encounters SIC products through retail, direct purchase,
          or as a gift. May have no prior knowledge of the imprint.
        situation: belongs
      failure:
        mode: product does not reach reader
        condition: fulfillment failure or distribution gap
        consequence: lost sale, no reader acquisition
        owner: SIC operations
```

---

## Choosing which views to apply

Not every descriptor requires all five views. Apply views where they add information an agent needs to build correctly — where leaving the view unspecified would produce an assumption.

A guide for common descriptors:

|Descriptor|Views most likely needed|
|---|---|
|`utility`|interface, failure|
|`model`|structure, behavior, failure|
|`component`|structure, behavior, failure|
|`actor`|interface, structure|
|`operation`|behavior, failure|
|`rule`|behavior, failure|
|`process`|behavior, failure, time|
|`integration`|interface, failure|
|`risk`|failure (by definition)|

These are starting points, not rules. A `model` may need a `time` view if it has a complex versioning history. A `component` may need an `interface` view if it exposes a public contract. Apply the view when the question it asks produces a meaningful answer for this system.

---

## Views and the coordinate model

Each view added to a descriptor populates a coordinate in the spec's coordinate space. A spec that applies all five views to all thirteen descriptors has a fully populated matrix — 65 coordinates. Most real specs populate a subset of this matrix, concentrating depth where the system's complexity and risk require it.

A sparse matrix is not a failure. It is a deliberate choice. What matters is that the choice is deliberate — that empty coordinates are empty because nothing essential lives there, not because the question was never asked.
